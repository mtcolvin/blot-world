Café Mexicali: Crafting a Vibrant Restaurant Identity

How I designed a complete brand identity system for a Californian-Mexican fusion restaurant, from concept to final deliverables.

Branding Identity Design Affinity Designer Visual Design

The Brief

Café Mexicali needed more than just a logo—they needed an identity that captured the vibrant intersection of Californian freshness and Mexican tradition. The goal was to create a brand that felt both authentic and modern, appealing to food enthusiasts who appreciate quality ingredients and bold flavors.

The challenge: How do you represent two rich culinary cultures without resorting to clichés? How do you make the brand feel fresh and contemporary while honoring traditional roots?

Research & Discovery

Understanding the Competitive Landscape

I began by analyzing successful restaurant brands in both the Mexican and Californian fusion spaces. What I noticed:

  • Many Mexican restaurants rely on predictable imagery: sombreros, cacti, and heavy serifs
  • California-focused brands tend toward minimalism, often losing personality
  • The sweet spot was unexplored: bold colors with clean, modern execution

Cultural Elements & Symbolism

I researched Mexican folk art, particularly papel picado (decorative paper banners) and traditional tile patterns. From California, I drew inspiration from mid-century modern design and the sun-drenched aesthetic of Southern California. The fusion of these elements would become the foundation of the brand.

Research Insight

The most authentic brands don't copy cultural elements—they reinterpret them through a modern lens. Respect tradition, but make it relevant.

Color Palette Development

Color would be the most immediate brand expression. After dozens of iterations, I landed on a palette that balanced warmth and energy:

  • Mexicali Orange (#F04923): The primary brand color—energetic, appetizing, and impossible to ignore
  • Sunflower Yellow (#F3B41B): California sunshine, warmth, and optimism
  • Adobe Brown (#89421E): Earthy grounding, connecting to traditional materials and authenticity
  • Dark Charcoal (#1A1A1A): Modern sophistication and premium quality

These colors aren't just decorative—they tell a story. Orange and yellow evoke the warmth of Mexican spices and California sun. Brown grounds the brand in authenticity. Dark charcoal adds the polish and premium feel.

Logo Design Process

Concept Sketches

I explored dozens of concepts, from literal interpretations (the Mexicali border, food imagery) to abstract marks. The breakthrough came when I stopped trying to represent what the restaurant serves and started thinking about how it makes people feel.

The Final Mark

The final logo features a stylized sunburst pattern inspired by traditional Mexican tile work, but executed with clean, geometric precision. The circular form suggests community, gathering, and the completeness of a meal shared.

The wordmark uses a custom-modified sans-serif typeface—clean and legible, but with subtle geometric modifications that echo the iconmark. The accent on the 'é' in 'Café' is emphasized as a design element, celebrating the authenticity of the name.

Building the Visual System

Typography Hierarchy

I established a clear typographic system:

  • Headlines: Bold geometric sans-serif for impact and clarity
  • Body Text: Clean, highly legible sans-serif for menus and descriptions
  • Accent Text: Condensed variant for labels and tags

Pattern Library

Extending the brand beyond the logo, I created a library of patterns inspired by traditional azulejo tiles. These patterns could be used as backgrounds, dividers, or decorative elements across all brand touchpoints—from menus to packaging to social media.

Iconography

A set of custom icons was developed for common restaurant needs: dietary markers (vegetarian, gluten-free, spicy), amenity icons (takeout, delivery, catering), and ingredient callouts. Each icon shares the same geometric language as the logo, maintaining visual cohesion.

Design Principles

Three core principles guided every design decision:

  • Bold but not Busy: Use color confidently, but give it breathing room
  • Modern but not Cold: Clean design doesn't have to lack personality
  • Consistent but not Boring: A flexible system that feels fresh in every application

Deliverables Package

The final brand package included everything needed for a comprehensive rollout:

Core Assets

  • Logo variations (full color, single color, reversed, icon-only)
  • Color palette with HEX, RGB, and CMYK values
  • Typography specifications and font files
  • Pattern library (8 unique tile patterns)
  • Icon set (24 custom icons)

Application Mockups

  • Menu designs (dine-in and takeout)
  • Business card and letterhead
  • Signage concepts (exterior and interior)
  • Packaging design (to-go containers, bags, cups)
  • Social media templates
  • Website homepage mockup

Brand Guidelines

A comprehensive 20-page brand guidelines document detailing proper logo usage, color applications, typography rules, and do's and don'ts. This ensures brand consistency as the restaurant grows and new materials are needed.

Technical Execution

Working in Affinity Designer

I chose Affinity Designer for this project for several reasons:

  • Vector Precision: Logos need to scale infinitely, from business cards to billboards
  • Performance: Handles complex artboards and mockups without lag
  • Export Flexibility: Easy export to multiple formats (SVG, PDF, PNG, EPS)
  • Non-subscription Model: One-time purchase makes it accessible to clients who may need to make minor edits

File Organization

Proper file organization is critical for brand work. I delivered:

  • Master design files with organized artboards
  • Exported assets in multiple formats
  • Print-ready files (CMYK, 300 DPI, with bleed)
  • Web-ready files (RGB, optimized for screen)
  • Editable templates for future use

Challenges & Solutions

Challenge: Standing Out Without Stereotypes

The Problem: Mexican restaurant branding often falls into visual clichés that feel dated or inauthentic.

The Solution: Instead of literally depicting Mexican imagery, I extracted principles from traditional design—vibrant color, geometric patterns, handcrafted feel—and reinterpreted them through modern design language.

Challenge: Versatility Across Applications

The Problem: The logo needs to work on a tiny business card and a massive storefront sign.

The Solution: Created multiple logo variations with different levels of detail. The icon-only version works at small sizes, while the full lockup shines at larger scales.

Challenge: Color Reproduction Across Media

The Problem: Colors look different on screen vs. print, and maintaining brand consistency is crucial.

The Solution: Defined specific color values for both RGB (screen) and CMYK (print) applications. Included color swatch files and Pantone references for professional printing.

Results & Reflections

The Café Mexicali brand successfully bridges two culinary worlds with visual confidence and cultural respect. The identity is bold enough to catch attention in a crowded market, yet sophisticated enough to convey quality and care.

This project reinforced several important lessons:

  • Research Pays Off: Understanding cultural context prevents superficial design
  • Systems Over Single Solutions: A cohesive brand system is more valuable than a pretty logo
  • Think in Applications: Design with real-world usage in mind from day one
  • Details Matter: Small refinements—adjusted kerning, perfect color balance—elevate good work to great work

Branding is storytelling through visual language. Every color choice, every curve in a letterform, every pattern repeat tells part of the story. Café Mexicali's story is one of fusion, vibrancy, and the joy of sharing great food with great people.
