The Eclipse

How a cosmic phenomenon shaped a philosophy

A Moment of Alignment

A solar eclipse is one of nature's most precise demonstrations of perspective and positioning. When the Moon passes between Earth and the Sun, it creates a moment where something relatively small—240,000 miles away and about 2,159 miles in diameter—can completely obscure something vastly larger—93 million miles away and 864,000 miles in diameter.

The math shouldn't work. The Sun is about 400 times larger than the Moon, but it's also about 400 times farther away. This cosmic coincidence means they appear nearly identical in size from our vantage point. It's a reminder that context, positioning, and perspective matter more than raw magnitude.

"The Moon is, in terms of distance, 1/400th the way to the Sun. And it's also 1/400th the size of the Sun. So it can exactly cover the Sun. It's a cosmic coincidence. If the Moon were a little bit farther away, it would not be able to create a total solar eclipse."

— Neil deGrasse Tyson

Small Things, Substantial Impact

This astronomical phenomenon became a guiding metaphor for how I approach work and creativity. You don't need to be the largest, loudest, or most powerful force to create meaningful change. What matters is alignment, timing, and understanding your position relative to the system you're operating within.

Individual Agency

One person, positioned correctly and acting with intention, can create disproportionate impact. Like the Moon blocking the Sun, scale isn't everything.

Context Over Size

The Moon doesn't change the Sun—it changes the observer's experience of it. Sometimes the goal isn't to be bigger, but to shift perspective.

Temporary Totality

Total solar eclipses last only a few minutes. Impact doesn't require permanence. Brief moments of clarity can change everything.

Precision Matters

The eclipse only works because of exact mathematical alignment. Small things making big impacts requires precision, not luck.

Translating Concept to Symbol

When designing the logo for BLOT.WORLD, I wanted to capture both the eclipse inspiration and create something that functioned as a personal mark. The solution was a dual-meaning design: eclipse viewing glasses merged with a stylized letter B.

Logo Breakdown

Eclipse Glasses

The protective eyewear needed to safely view a solar eclipse. Represents observation, protection, and the tools we use to witness rare moments.

Letter B

My personal initial, stylized to form the frame and lenses. Creates a monogram that's both personal and conceptual.

Dual Reading

The design works whether you see glasses first or the letter B first. Both interpretations are valid—context determines meaning.

Why Astronomy?

My fascination with astronomy comes from its unique combination of humbling scale and precise mathematics. Space is incomprehensibly vast, yet we can predict celestial events centuries in advance with incredible accuracy. It's both chaos and order, randomness and precision.

Eclipses specifically represent those rare moments where the cosmic machinery becomes visible to the naked eye. For a few minutes, the mechanics of the solar system reveal themselves in a way that requires no instruments, no education, no expertise. Just look up.

That accessibility combined with precision is what I aim for in my work—creating things that are technically sound but experientially immediate. You shouldn't need expertise to appreciate good design, clear code, or thoughtful photography. The technical foundation should enable the experience, not gatekeep it.

Blotting Out the Sun

The phrase "Blot Out The Sun" has aggressive connotations—it appears in ancient battle stories, usually as a threat about arrows darkening the sky. But in the context of eclipses, it's not about destruction. It's about revelation.

When the Moon blocks the Sun during totality, we can finally see the Sun's corona—the wispy, ethereal atmosphere that's normally invisible due to the Sun's overwhelming brightness. Blocking the light reveals what was hidden. Sometimes you need to obscure the obvious to see what's actually there.

In creative work, this translates to questioning assumptions, removing noise, and stripping away what's expected to reveal something more essential. It's about having the confidence to cover what everyone else is looking at in order to show them something they've been missing.

The Next Eclipse

Solar eclipses happen somewhere on Earth every 18 months on average, but total eclipses visible from any specific location are rare—roughly once every 375 years. The next total solar eclipse visible from the United States will occur on August 23, 2044.

That rarity makes each one significant. When you witness totality, you're experiencing something most people who've ever lived never saw. It's a reminder that some moments only happen once, and positioning yourself to witness them matters.

This site exists as an archive and observatory—a place to document work, track evolution, and preserve the moments when small efforts aligned to create something larger. Not because I think my work will blot out anyone's sun, but because I believe in showing up, positioning carefully, and making the small things count.
